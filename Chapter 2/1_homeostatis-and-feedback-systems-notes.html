Homeostatis and Feedback Systems

▶What is homeostasis?
  • Homeostasis is the maintenance of a relatively constant volume, composition, and temperature of the body's internal environment.
  • For cells to function properly, their internal contents must be kept within a very narrow range.
▶How is homeostasis maintained in an "open system"?
  • The human body is an "open system" where substances are constantly added (e.g., food) and removed (e.g., waste).
  • Homeostasis is achieved through a steady-state balance, where the rate of input must equal the rate of output.
▶What is required for a steady-state balance?
  • Set Point: A reference value that the system tries to maintain.
  • Sensors (or Receptors): Structures that monitor for deviations from the set point.
  • Control Center: Receives input from the sensors and sends out commands.
  • Effectors: Organs or tissues that receive commands and produce a response to restore the balance.
▶What is the difference between negative and positive feedback?
  • Negative Feedback: The most common type of feedback loop. The effector's response reverses or counteracts the original stimulus, bringing the system back to its set point. An example is the lowering of high blood pressure.
  • Positive Feedback: A less common type. The effector's response enhances or reinforces the original stimulus, pushing the system further from its starting state until a specific event occurs. An example is the release of oxytocin to increase uterine contractions during childbirth.
▶How is water balance maintained?
  • Water balance is a key part of homeostasis, where water input must equal water output.
  • Sensor: Cells in the hypothalamus of the brain monitor body fluid osmolality (the set point).
  • Effector Signals: If water levels are low, the hypothalamus generates two signals:
    1. A neural signal creating the sensation of thirst (to increase input).
    2. A hormonal signal releasing ADH, which tells the kidneys to excrete less water (to decrease output).
▶What are the body's main fluid compartments?
  • Total Body Water (TBW): Makes up about 60% of total body weight. For a 70 kg person, this is about 42 Liters.
  • Intracellular Fluid (ICF): The fluid inside the cells. This is the largest compartment, containing about 2/3 of the total body water (approx. 28 L).
  • Extracellular Fluid (ECF): The fluid outside the cells, representing the body's internal environment. It contains about 1/3 of the total body water (approx. 14 L).
▶How is the Extracellular Fluid (ECF) subdivided?
  • The ECF is divided into two main sub-compartments:
    1. Interstitial Fluid (ISF): The fluid that directly surrounds the cells in the tissues. It makes up about 3/4 of the ECF (approx. 10.5 L).
    2. Plasma: The fluid component of blood, contained within the blood vessels. It makes up about 1/4 of the ECF (approx. 3.5 L).

Summary

Homeostasis is the body's ability to maintain a stable internal state through feedback systems. The body's water is divided into two main compartments: the larger intracellular fluid (ICF) and the smaller extracellular fluid (ECF), which is further divided into interstitial fluid and plasma. Maintaining the volume and composition of these compartments is a fundamental goal of homeostasis.

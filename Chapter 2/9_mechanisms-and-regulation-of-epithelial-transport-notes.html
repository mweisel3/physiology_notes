Mechanisms and Regulation of Epithelial Transport

▶How does the structure of an epithelial cell enable vectorial transport?
  • In asymmetrical epithelial cells, transport proteins are segregated to different membrane domains.
  • For example, Na⁺ entry channels may be located only on the apical membrane, while the Na⁺,K⁺-ATPase pump is located only on the basolateral membrane.
  • The pump on the basolateral side creates a steep electrochemical gradient that pulls Na⁺ into the cell through the apical channel, resulting in a net, directional movement of Na⁺ across the entire cell.
▶What is the difference between absorption and secretion?
  • Absorption: This is vectorial transport from the apical side to the basolateral side of the epithelium. An example is the absorption of nutrients from the intestine.
  • Secretion: This is vectorial transport from the basolateral side to the apical side.
  • Both processes are typically dependent on the energy from ATP, often via the Na⁺,K⁺-ATPase pump.
▶How does the paracellular pathway relate to transcellular transport?
  • The paracellular pathway (movement between cells) is a passive route for ions and water.
  • The driving forces for this pathway—the transepithelial voltage and concentration gradients—are established by the active transcellular transport that occurs through the cells.
  • In this way, the energy-requiring transcellular route sets up the conditions for the passive paracellular route.
▶How does water move across an epithelium?
  • Water movement across an epithelium is always a passive process, driven by osmotic gradients.
  • These osmotic gradients are created by the active transport of solutes across the cell.
  • Water can move both through the cells (transcellular pathway, via aquaporins) and between the cells (paracellular pathway).
▶How is epithelial transport regulated?
  • Epithelial transport is tightly regulated by neural, hormonal (e.g., aldosterone), and local paracrine signals to maintain homeostasis.
  • Cells can respond to these signals in three main ways:
    1. By adding or removing transporters from the membrane via exocytosis or endocytosis.
    2. By changing the activity of existing transporters (e.g., opening or closing a channel).
    3. By synthesizing entirely new transporter proteins.

Summary

The specific placement of transporters on either the apical or basolateral membrane allows epithelial cells to perform directional vectorial transport (absorption or secretion). This active, transcellular transport creates electrical and chemical gradients that can then drive the passive paracellular transport of other substances like water and ions. The entire process is tightly regulated by hormones and the nervous system to meet the body's changing homeostatic needs.

Starling Forces and Capillary Exchange

▶What are Starling forces?
  • Starling forces are a set of four pressures that control the movement of fluid across the walls of capillaries.
  • They determine the balance between filtration (fluid moving out of the capillary) and absorption (fluid moving into the capillary).
▶What are the two main types of forces involved?
  1. Hydrostatic Pressure: This is the physical "pushing" pressure exerted by the fluid.
    • Capillary hydrostatic pressure (Pc) is the blood pressure that pushes fluid out of the capillary.
  2. Oncotic Pressure: This is the osmotic "pulling" pressure created by proteins (primarily albumin) in the fluid.
    • Plasma oncotic pressure (πc) is the force that pulls fluid into the capillary.
▶What do the variables in the Starling equation represent?

The Starling equation is: Fluid flow = Kf [(Pc − Pi) − (πc − πi)]

  • Kf: The filtration constant (a measure of the capillary's permeability and surface area).
  • Pc: Capillary hydrostatic pressure (pushes fluid out).
  • Pi: Interstitial fluid hydrostatic pressure (pushes fluid in).
  • πc: Plasma oncotic pressure (pulls fluid in).
  • πi: Interstitial fluid oncotic pressure (pulls fluid out).
▶How do these forces determine fluid movement?
  • The balance between these forces changes along the length of a capillary.
  • At the Arterial End: Hydrostatic pressure is high and stronger than oncotic pressure. This results in a net filtration, where fluid and nutrients are pushed out of the capillary into the interstitial space.
  • At the Venous End: Blood pressure drops, so hydrostatic pressure is now weaker than oncotic pressure. This results in a net absorption, where fluid and waste products are pulled back into the capillary.

Summary

Fluid exchange across capillaries is governed by Starling forces, which can be expressed in a formula. The balance between the outward push of hydrostatic pressure (Pc) and the inward pull of oncotic pressure (πc) determines whether fluid is filtered out of the capillary (at the arterial end) or reabsorbed back in (at the venous end).

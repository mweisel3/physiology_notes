Osmotic Equilibrium and Fluid Shifts

▶What is osmotic equilibrium?
  • Osmotic equilibrium is the state where all body fluid compartments (ICF and ECF) have the same osmolality, even though their chemical compositions are very different.
  • If the osmolality of one compartment changes, water will quickly shift between compartments to restore this balance.
  • Because of this, measuring the osmolality of blood plasma allows doctors to know the osmolality of all other body fluids.
▶⭐What happens when an Isotonic solution is added to the ECF?
  • Adding an isotonic solution (e.g., 0.9% NaCl) increases the volume of the ECF only.
  • Because the solution has the same osmolality as the body's fluids, no osmotic gradient is created.
  • As a result, there is no net movement of water, and the volume of the intracellular fluid (ICF) remains unchanged.
▶⭐What happens when a Hypotonic solution is added to the ECF?
  • Adding a hypotonic solution (e.g., 0.45% NaCl) first dilutes the ECF, lowering its osmolality.
  • This creates an osmotic gradient that causes water to move from the ECF into the more concentrated ICF.
  • At the new equilibrium, both the ECF and ICF have a lower osmolality and a larger volume than the normal state.
▶⭐What happens when a Hypertonic solution is added to the ECF?
  • Adding a hypertonic solution (e.g., 3% NaCl) first concentrates the ECF, raising its osmolality.
  • This creates an osmotic gradient that pulls water out of the ICF and into the more concentrated ECF.
  • At the new equilibrium, both compartments have a higher osmolality; the ICF volume has decreased, while the ECF volume has increased.
▶How is the volume of a fluid compartment measured?
  • The volume is measured using the Indicator-Dilution Method.
  • A known amount of an indicator dye is injected into a compartment and allowed to spread evenly.
  • The volume is then calculated by dividing the total amount of indicator injected by its final measured concentration.
  • Volume = Amount Injected / Final Concentration
▶What is osmotherapy?
  • Osmotherapy is a medical treatment that uses an osmotic agent to create an osmotic gradient, which pulls excess water out of a specific tissue.
▶What clinical problem is osmotherapy used for?
  • It is often used to treat cerebral edema (swelling of the brain), which can result from strokes or neurosurgical procedures.
  • This swelling is dangerous because it increases intracranial pressure, which can disrupt neuronal function and lead to coma or death.
▶How does osmotheropy work to reduce brain swelling?
  1. An osmotic agent that cannot cross the blood-brain barrier (BBB), such as the sugar mannitol, is given to the patient intravenously.
  2. This agent increases the osmolality (concentration) of the blood.
  3. Because water can freely cross the BBB but mannitol cannot, an osmotic gradient is created between the blood and the brain tissue.
  4. This gradient pulls excess fluid from the brain tissue into the blood vessels via osmosis.
  5. This process shrinks the brain, which reduces the dangerous intracranial pressure.

Summary

The body's fluid compartments are normally in osmotic equilibrium, meaning they have the same osmolality. The infusion of isotonic, hypotonic, or hypertonic solutions predictably alters the volume and osmolality of the ECF and ICF by causing water to either stay put or shift between them. The volume of these compartments can be measured using the indicator-dilution method.

Central Nervous System (CNS)

▶What is "gray matter" and "white matter"?
  • Gray Matter: These are regions in the CNS with a high concentration of neuron cell bodies (somas) and dendrites. This is where most of the information processing and synaptic integration occurs.
  • White Matter: These are regions composed primarily of myelinated axons. The white, fatty myelin is what gives this tissue its color. White matter acts as the "wiring" of the CNS, transmitting signals between different gray matter regions.
▶What are the two main configurations of gray matter?
  1. Nucleus: A cluster or group of neuron cell bodies located deep within the brain or spinal cord. (Note: In the PNS, a similar cluster is called a ganglion).
  2. Cortex: A layered sheet of neurons, typically found on the surface of the brain, like the cerebral cortex or cerebellar cortex.
▶What is the difference between a tract and a pathway?
  • Tract: A bundle of axons (white matter) that travel together from one specific location to another within the CNS. Tracts are often named for their origin and destination (e.g., the spinocerebellar tract runs from the spinal cord to the cerebellum).
  • Pathway: A more functional term describing a series of interconnected neurons that work together to transmit a specific type of information (e.g., the auditory pathway). A pathway can involve multiple tracts and nuclei.
▶What are the two broad categories of neurons in the CNS?
  • Projection Neurons: These neurons have long axons that leave the nucleus or cortical area where their cell body resides and project to a distant region of the CNS. They form the tracts of the nervous system.
  • Local Interneurons: These neurons have short axons that begin and end within the same structure. They are involved in local processing and computations within that specific brain region.
▶How is the spinal cord organized?
  • The spinal cord is a long, tubular structure that is segmented into regions corresponding to the vertebrae: cervical, thoracic, lumbar, and sacral.
  • In cross-section, the gray matter is on the inside (in a butterfly shape) and the white matter is on the outside.
  • The dorsal horn (posterior part of the gray matter) primarily receives and processes sensory information.
  • The ventral horn (anterior part of the gray matter) is primarily motor, containing the cell bodies of motor neurons that send signals out to the muscles.
▶How is the spinal cord divided, and what are the primary roles of each section?
  • The spinal cord is subdivided into five regions. The nerves that exit from each region are generally responsible for providing sensation and motor control to specific parts of the body.
Section Name Number of Segments Primary Role / Areas Controlled
Cervical 8 Controls the head, neck, diaphragm, shoulders, arms, and hands.
Thoracic 12 Controls chest muscles, abdominal muscles, and some back muscles. Nerves also connect to internal organs.
Lumbar 5 Controls the lower back, hips, and front of the legs.
Sacral 5 Controls the back of the legs, genitals, bowel, and bladder function.
Coccygeal 1 Provides sensation to the skin around the tailbone.
▶What is Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF)?
  • Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF) is a clear fluid that fills the ventricular system inside the brain and the subarachnoid space surrounding the brain and spinal cord.
  • The brain essentially "floats" in the CSF, which helps to cushion it from mechanical forces and injury.
  • The composition of CSF reflects the composition of the brain's extracellular fluid.
▶Where is CSF produced and how does it circulate?
  • CSF is primarily produced by specialized tissue called the choroid plexus, which is located within the brain's ventricles.
  • The fluid circulates through a series of interconnected spaces:
    1. It flows from the two lateral ventricles into the third ventricle.
    2. It then passes through the cerebral aqueduct to the fourth ventricle.
    3. Finally, it exits the ventricular system into the subarachnoid space to surround the entire CNS.
  • The body produces about 0.35 mL of CSF per minute, turning over the entire volume more than three times a day.
▶How does CSF differ from blood?
  • CSF is a filtrate of blood, but its composition is different. Compared to blood, CSF has:
    • Lower concentrations of K⁺, glucose, and protein.
    • Higher concentrations of Na⁺ and Cl⁻.
    • Practically no blood cells.
  • These differences are maintained by specialized transport mechanisms in the choroid plexus.
▶What is the Blood-Brain Barrier (BBB)?
  • The blood-brain barrier is a highly selective barrier that separates the circulating blood from the brain's extracellular fluid.
  • It is formed by the endothelial cells of the CNS capillaries, which are linked by very tight junctions, and is supported by the foot processes of astrocytes.
  • The BBB strictly controls the passage of substances, protecting the neurons from extreme variations in blood composition and from potentially harmful substances.
▶What are the three layers of the meninges, from outermost to innermost?
  1. Dura Mater: The outermost and toughest layer. It is a thick, durable membrane that provides a strong protective covering for the brain.
  2. Arachnoid Mater: The middle layer, located just beneath the dura mater. It is a web-like structure. The space beneath it, the subarachnoid space, is filled with cerebrospinal fluid (CSF).
  3. Pia Mater: The innermost layer. It is a delicate membrane that adheres tightly to the surface of the brain, following all of its folds and contours.

Summary

The Central Nervous System is organized into gray matter (for processing) and white matter (for communication). Gray matter is arranged into nuclei and cortices, which contain projection neurons (for long-distance signaling) and interneurons (for local processing). The spinal cord, a key part of the CNS, has a distinct organization with a central gray matter core, where the dorsal horn processes sensory input and the ventral horn controls motor output.

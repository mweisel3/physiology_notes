Glial Cells of the Nervous System

▶What are glial cells?
  • Glial cells (or neuroglia) are the non-neuronal support cells of the nervous system.
  • They are the most numerous cell type in the central nervous system (CNS), outnumbering neurons by about ten to one.
  • Their primary role is to provide metabolic and physical support to neurons, regulate the environment of the nervous system, and shape the flow of signals.
▶What are astrocytes and their main functions?
  • Astrocytes are the most common type of glial cell in the CNS. They are star-shaped cells that make contact with neurons, synapses, and blood vessels.
  • Their main functions include:
    • Metabolic Support: They provide nutrients like lactate to neurons.
    • Environmental Regulation: They buffer the extracellular space by taking up excess ions (like K⁺) and neurotransmitters (like glutamate).
    • Structural Support: They provide mechanical support to the CNS tissue.
    • Synaptic Insulation: Their processes surround synapses, isolating them from neighboring connections.
    • Injury Response: Following an injury, they can become "reactive" and form a glial scar to isolate the damaged area.
▶What is the primary function of oligodendrocytes and Schwann cells?
  • Their primary function is to form the myelin sheath that insulates axons. Myelin is a multilayered wrapping of the glial cell's membrane that allows for much faster conduction of electrical signals.
  • Oligodendrocytes: Create the myelin sheath in the Central Nervous System (CNS). A single oligodendrocyte can myelinate segments of multiple different axons.
  • Schwann Cells: Create the myelin sheath in the Peripheral Nervous System (PNS). A single Schwann cell myelinates a segment of only one axon.
▶What is microglia?
  • Microglia are the primary immune cells of the CNS.
  • They constantly sample their environment with mobile processes.
  • Their functions include:
    • Phagocytosis: They clean up cellular debris and pathogens.
    • Immune Response: They become activated in response to injury or pathogens.
    • Synaptic Remodeling: They play a role in removing or reshaping synapses, which is important for brain plasticity.
▶What are the other types of glial cells?
  • Ependymal Cells: These cells form the epithelial lining of the brain's ventricles and are responsible for producing cerebrospinal fluid (CSF).
  • Satellite Cells: Found in the PNS, they act similarly to astrocytes by regulating the microenvironment around the cell bodies of neurons in ganglia.
▶Why are glial cells a common source of brain tumors?
  • Most mature neurons are post-mitotic, meaning they do not divide.
  • In contrast, glial cells (or their precursors) retain the ability to divide throughout life.
  • Because of this, most primary brain tumors in adults are derived from glial cells, such as astrocytomas (from astrocytes) and oligodendrogliomas (from oligodendrocytes).

Summary

Glial cells are the essential support network of the nervous system. Astrocytes manage the environment and provide metabolic support in the CNS. Oligodendrocytes (in the CNS) and Schwann cells (in the PNS) create the insulating myelin sheath that speeds up nerve signals. Microglia act as the resident immune cells, while ependymal cells produce cerebrospinal fluid. Unlike neurons, glia can divide, making them the primary source of brain tumors.

The Neuron

▶What are the three main parts of a neuron?
  • A neuron is composed of three main parts:
    1. The Cell Body (Soma): The metabolic and genetic center of the cell.
    2. Dendrites: Branching extensions that are the primary receivers of signals from other neurons.
    3. The Axon: A single, long extension that transmits the neuron's output signal to other cells.
▶What is the function of the cell body (soma)?
  • The soma contains the nucleus and a well-developed biosynthetic system (including Nissl bodies, which are stacks of rough ER) for producing all the necessary components for the neuron's function.
  • It is also a site for receiving synaptic inputs, though it receives fewer signals than the dendrites.
▶What is the function of dendrites?
  • Dendrites are tapering extensions that massively increase the surface area of the neuron, allowing it to receive signals from many other cells. They can account for over 90% of a neuron's surface area.
  • A neuron's entire set of dendrites is called its "dendritic tree."
  • Dendrites are the primary site for receiving synaptic inputs (afferent signals) from other neurons.
▶What is the function of the axon?
  • The axon is a single extension that transmits the neuron's output signal.
  • It begins at a specialized region of the soma called the axon hillock. The first part of the axon is the initial segment, which is typically where action potentials are generated.
  • The axon can be covered in a myelin sheath, an insulating layer that speeds up signal transmission. The gaps in this sheath are called the Nodes of Ranvier.
  • The axon ends in a spray of branches called the terminal arborization, where it forms synapses with other cells.
▶What is neuronal pathfinding and what structure is responsible for it?
  • Neuronal pathfinding is the process by which a developing neuron extends its axon to find and connect with its correct target cell, which could be far away.
  • The structure responsible for guiding the axon is the growth cone, a specialized, mobile structure at the tip of the growing axon.
▶How does the growth cone guide the axon?
  • The growth cone "feels" or "smells" its way through the environment by responding to chemical guidance cues.
  • It has extensions called filopodia that explore the surroundings. These cues can either attract or repel the growth cone, directing the axon along the correct path to its target.
▶What is axonal transport and why is it necessary?
  • Since all synthesis of proteins and organelles happens in the soma, a special transport system is needed to move these materials down the long axon to the terminals. Simple diffusion would be far too slow.
  • This process, called axonal transport, moves materials along microtubule "tracks" using motor proteins like kinesin and dynein, a process that requires ATP.
▶What are the two directions of axonal transport?
  1. Anterograde Transport: Movement from the soma toward the axon terminal. This supplies the terminal with synaptic vesicles, enzymes, and other necessary components.
  2. Retrograde Transport: Movement from the axon terminal back to the soma. This returns old materials for recycling and can also transport signals from the periphery back to the cell body.
▶How is axonal transport relevant to disease?
  • Some viruses and toxins can hijack the axonal transport system to travel through the nervous system.
  • Herpes Zoster (Shingles): The chickenpox virus can lie dormant in a neuron's soma for years. When reactivated, it travels via anterograde transport down the axon to the skin, causing shingles.
  • Tetanus Toxin: The toxin is taken up by axon terminals and travels via retrograde transport to the central nervous system, where it can cause severe muscle spasms.
▶What is the "Neuron Doctrine"?
  • Championed by Santiago Ramón y Cajal, the Neuron Doctrine is the fundamental principle that neurons are individual, discrete cells that communicate with each other at specialized junctions called synapses.
  • This was in contrast to Camillo Golgi's theory that the nervous system was a continuous, interconnected network (a reticulum). Cajal's view was proven correct and is the basis of modern neuroscience.
▶How are the different parts of a neuron functionally specialized?
  • The specialization of each part of the neuron is due to its unique morphology and the specific types of ion channels it contains.
  • Dendrites and Soma: These regions are specialized for receiving signals. Their membranes are rich in ligand-gated ion channels, which respond to chemical neurotransmitters released by other neurons.
  • Axon: This region is specialized for propagating signals over long distances. Its membrane is rich in voltage-gated ion channels, which allow for the generation and transmission of the action potential.

Summary

The neuron is the fundamental unit of the nervous system, composed of dendrites (for input), a soma (for metabolic control), and an axon (for output). During development, the axon is guided to its target by a mobile growth cone in a process called pathfinding. A mature neuron relies on axonal transport to move materials from the soma to the distant terminals, and its function depends on the specialized distribution of ion channels: ligand-gated channels on dendrites receive signals, while voltage-gated channels on the axon propagate them.

Osmosis and Tonicity

▶What is osmosis?
  • Osmosis is the spontaneous and passive movement of water molecules across a semi-permeable membrane.
  • It does not require the cell to expend energy.
  • The driving force for this movement is the difference in osmotic pressure across the membrane.
  • Water moves from an area of low solute concentration to an area of high solute concentration.
▶What is osmotic pressure?
  • Osmotic pressure is the force driving osmosis, and it is determined by the total number of solute molecules dissolved in a solution.
  • It is not affected by the size, mass, or chemical nature of the solute particles.
  • It is calculated using van't Hoff's law: π = nCRT.
▶What is osmotic concentration?
  • Osmotic concentration, or osmolarity, is the measure of the total number of solute particles per liter of solution.
  • This is different from molar concentration because it accounts for molecules that dissociate (split apart) in water.
  • For example, a 1 mol/L solution of MgCl₂ has an osmolarity of 3 osmol/L because each molecule splits into three particles (one Mg²⁺ and two Cl⁻ ions). For a substance like glucose that doesn't split, its osmolarity is equal to its molarity.
▶What is tonicity?
  • Tonicity describes the effect a solution has on a cell's volume.
  • It depends on the concentration of solutes that cannot cross the cell membrane (non-penetrating solutes).
  • Isotonic Solution: The cell's volume does not change.
  • Hypotonic Solution: The cell swells as water enters.
  • Hypertonic Solution: The cell shrinks as water leaves.
▶What is oncotic pressure?
  • Oncotic pressure is the specific term for the osmotic pressure generated by large molecules, especially proteins, in a solution.
  • The actual oncotic pressure exerted by proteins in plasma is higher than what is predicted by van't Hoff's law.

Summary

Osmosis is the passive movement of water across a membrane, driven by osmotic pressure, which depends on the total number of solute particles (osmotic concentration or osmolarity). Tonicity is a functional term describing how a solution's concentration of non-penetrating solutes affects cell volume, causing it to remain stable (isotonic), swell (hypotonic), or shrink (hypertonic).

Membrane Transport Proteins and Channels

▶Why is transport necessary?
  • Normal cell function requires the continuous movement of water and solutes across the membrane
  • The membrane's hydrophobic core is an effective barrier, so specific membrane transporters are needed to provide a pathway for most molecules.
▶⭐What are the 4 classes of transporters?
  1. Water Channels (תעלת מים)
  2. Ion Channels (תעלת יוונית)
  3. Solute Carries (נשאי מומסים)
  4. ATP-Dependent Transporters (נושאים התלוים ב ATP)
▶What are water channels?
  • main routes for water to enter and exit the cell - they can traverse the membrane, but slowly because of polarity
  • Integral proteins that allow the passive movement of water across the membrane
  • Transport rate is very high, up to 109 molecules/sec
▶How do water channels work?
  • Passive
  • Have a narrow bottleneck so that it restricts the size of molecules that can pass through
  • Cations line the channel to move the slightly polar molecules down
  • Typically have 4 proteins that need each other to be the right shape to work, but water molecules can pass through any of them
▶What are ion channels?
  • Found in all cell types, they allow ions to pass through the membrane
  • They are selective
  • They fluctuate between an open and closed state, a process called gating
  • Transport rate is fast, between 106 - 108 molecules/sec
▶What are the 4 kinds of ion channel gates?
  • outer-ligand gate - it opens and closes base a protein that functions like a "key"
  • inner-ligand gate
  • mechanical - if the membrane stretches or shrinks the gate with open/close
  • voltage (מתח) - if outside the membrane has a positive charge and inside is negative, it can open the gate
▶What are solute carries?
  • A very large group of transporters that bind to solutes and undergo a conformational change to move them across the membrane .
  • They work much slower than channels, at a rate of 102 - 104 molecules/sec.
▶What are the 3 types of solute carries?

Uniporters - 1 molecule at a time Symporters - 2+ Molecule in the same direction Antiporters - 2+ molecules in opposite directions

▶How do solute carries work?

The solute binds to the carries, changing the carrier's shape. This releases energy which moves the transporter, it then releases the solute and returns to its original shape

▶What are ATP-Dependent Transporters

Use energy from ATP hydrolysis, which moves the molecules across the membrane (even against the gradient).

▶What is diffusion?

Goes from high to low concentration, it does not require energy

Summary

The cell membrane's selective permeability is managed by four major classes of transport proteins. Water channels (Aquaporins) and Ion channels allow for very fast, passive movement of water and ions, respectively. Solute carriers work more slowly by binding to molecules and changing shape, operating as uniporters, symporters, or antiporters. ATP-dependent transporters act as pumps, using ATP to move substances, often against their gradient.

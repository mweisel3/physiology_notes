▶What is selective permeability and what can cross the membrane?

Selective permeability means the membrane allows some substances to pass while preventing the passage of others. A molecule's ability to cross the membrane depends on its size, charge, and lipid solubility.

  • High Permeability (Simple Diffusion):
    • Small, nonpolar molecules like blood gases (O₂, N₂, CO₂) cross easily.
    • Small, lipid-soluble molecules like fatty acids and steroid hormones also pass through directly.
  • Limited Permeability:
    • Small, uncharged polar molecules like water, urea, and glycerol can pass, but their movement is restricted.
  • Very Low / No Permeability (Require Transporters):
    • Larger, uncharged polar molecules like glucose cannot cross on their own.
    • Charged molecules like amino acids and nucleotides are blocked.
    • Inorganic ions like Na⁺, K⁺, and Ca²⁺ are blocked.
    • Macromolecules like proteins and nucleic acids cannot pass through.

Summary

The cell membrane is selectively permeable, meaning it allows small, nonpolar, and lipid-soluble molecules to pass freely while blocking larger, polar, and charged substances. These blocked substances require transport proteins to cross the membrane.

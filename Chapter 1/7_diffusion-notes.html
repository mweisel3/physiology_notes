Diffusion

▶What is diffusion?

Diffusion is the spontaneous movement of molecules from an area of high concentration to an area of low concentration.

▶What is Fick's Law?

Fick's Law is the formula that describes the rate of diffusion (J): J = -DA(ΔC/ΔX) Where: D = Diffusion coefficient (accounts for molecule size, temperature, and viscosity). A = The area across which diffusion is occurring. ΔC = The concentration difference (gradient). ΔX = The diffusion distance.

▶What factors increase the rate of diffusion?

Smaller molecule size. Higher temperature. Larger concentration gradient. Lower viscosity of the medium.

▶How does the cell membrane affect diffusion?

The cell membrane acts as a barrier that limits diffusion. Hydrophobic (lipid-soluble) molecules can cross the lipid bilayer relatively easily. Hydrophilic (water-soluble) molecules find it difficult to cross without help. Because of this, most biologically important molecules require a transporter protein to cross the membrane efficiently.

▶Where is diffusion important in the body?

Diffusion is critical for many physiological processes, including: Gas exchange in the lungs.

Summary

Diffusion is the passive movement of molecules down their concentration gradient, a process described by Fick's Law. The rate of diffusion is faster for smaller molecules at higher temperatures. The cell's plasma membrane is a significant barrier to diffusion, especially for water-soluble molecules, making it a key regulator of what enters and leaves the cell. This process is fundamental to functions like gas exchange.

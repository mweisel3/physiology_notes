Membrane Potential and Electrochemical Gradient

▶What is membrane potential?
  • Membrane potential (Vm) is the electrical voltage difference across the cell membrane.
  • In most cells, the inside is negatively charged relative to the outside.
  • This charge separation occurs right at the membrane, with negative charges lined up on the inner surface and positive charges on the outer surface.
  • The bulk solutions on either side of the membrane remain electrically neutral.
▶What is the electrochemical gradient?
  • The electrochemical gradient is the total driving force that causes a molecule to move across the membrane.
  • It is a measure of the free energy available to perform the work of transport.
  • It has two components:
    1. Chemical Gradient: The force from the difference in the molecule's concentration across the membrane.
    2. Electrical Gradient: The force from the membrane potential (the charge difference) across the membrane.
  • For uncharged molecules like glucose, only the chemical gradient matters. For charged ions like K+, both the chemical and electrical gradients contribute to the total driving force.

Summary

The cell membrane maintains an electrical voltage called the membrane potential, with the inside typically being negative. The movement of molecules is driven by the electrochemical gradient, which is the sum of the chemical (concentration) gradient and, for charged molecules, the electrical (voltage) gradient.

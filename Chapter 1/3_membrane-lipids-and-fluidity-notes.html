Membrane Lipids and Fluidity

▶ What are the main types of lipids in the membrane?
Phospholipids Cholesterol Glycolipid
The most abundant, forming the basic bylaer structure An essential molecule that helps stabilize the membrane Lipids with attached carbohydrate groups, less are less common, but still present

▶ What is the role of cholesterol
  • It's a critical component found in both the inner and outer leaflets of the membrane .
  • It stabilizes and stiffens the membrane at normal body temperature .
  • Cholesterol can make up as much as 50% of the lipids in the membrane.
▶ Is the membrane lipid composition symmetrical?

No: Outer leaflet: Predominantly Phosphatidylcholine and Sphingomyelin Inner leaflet: Predominantly Phosphatidylethanolamine, Phosphatidylserine, and Phosphatidylinositol

▶ Is the membrane static?

The lipid bilayer is not a rigid structure but a dynamic, fluid one where components are in constant motion. This is often called the fluid mosaic model.

▶ What kind of movements can the lipids make?
Movement Type Movement Description Requires Energy
Horizontal (Lateral) Sideways within own layer ❌
Transpose (Traverse Diffusion/Flip Flop) Moves from one leaflet to another ✅
Rotational/Flexion Fatty Acid tails constantly rotating around σ bonds ❌
▶ What factors affect fluidity?
  • Temperature (high temperature increases fluidity)
  • Lipid composition - alkenes (double bonded C) increases fluidity because the "kink" in the tail limits tight packing
  • Cholesterol - Moderates fluidity, making the membrane less fluid at high temperatures and more fluid at low temperates

Summary

The cell membrane is a fluid mosaic composed mainly of phospholipids, cholesterol, and glycolipids. These lipids are distributed asymmetrically between the two leaflets. Cholesterol is a key component that provides stability. The membrane's fluidity, which allows for the movement of its components, is primarily determined by temperature and the presence of unsaturated fatty acids.

Active vs Passive Transport

▶What is the difference between passive and active transport?
  • Passive Transport is the movement of molecules down their electrochemical gradient. It is a spontaneous process that does not require the cell to expend metabolic energy.
  • Active Transport is the movement of molecules against their electrochemical gradient. This "uphill" movement is not spontaneous and requires an input of energy.
▶What are the types of passive transport?
  • Simple Diffusion: Small, lipid-soluble molecules pass directly through the lipid bilayer without help.
  • Facilitated Diffusion: Molecules cross the membrane with the help of a transport protein, but still move down their electrochemical gradient. This includes movement through:
    • Channels: Pores that can be open or gated (e.g., ion channels, aquaporins).
    • Carriers (Uniporters): Proteins that bind to a molecule and change shape to carry it across the membrane one at a time.
▶What are the types of active transport?
  1. Primary Active Transport: The transport protein directly uses energy from the hydrolysis of ATP to push molecules against their gradient. The Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase pump is a classic example.
  2. Secondary Active Transport: This process uses the energy stored in an electrochemical gradient of one molecule to drive the transport of another molecule against its own gradient. It does not use ATP directly. This is also called "coupled transport" and is carried out by:
    • Symporters: Move two substances in the same direction.
    • Antiporters: Move two substances in opposite directions.
▶How do primary and secondary active transport work together?
  • These two systems are fundamentally linked.
  • A primary pump (like the Na⁺/K⁺ pump) uses ATP to create a steep ion gradient (e.g., high Na⁺ outside the cell).
  • A secondary transporter (like the Na⁺-glucose symporter) then uses the energy of that Na⁺ gradient (as Na⁺ flows back into the cell) to pull glucose into the cell against its own gradient.
  • Essentially, the cell spends ATP in one place to create a gradient that can be used as an energy source for transport in another place.
▶What is the difference between a transporter vs a channel

A transporter changes shape, whereas a channel does not (although it can be gated or open).

Summary

Cellular transport is either passive or active. Passive transport, including simple and facilitated diffusion, moves substances down their gradient without energy. Active transport moves substances against their gradient and requires energy. This energy can come directly from ATP (primary active transport) or from the power of another ion's gradient that was previously established by a primary pump (secondary active transport).
